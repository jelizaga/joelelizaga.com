Epidural Test Doses
The epidural test dose serves to identify both as well as intravascular placement of the epidural catheter.

Testing for Subarachnoid Placement
Lidocaine 45-60 mg (3 ml of 1.5-2% lidocaine) will produce a dense sensory and motor block when placed intrathecally within 3 minutes. There are case reports of total spinal anesthesia occuring after doses as small as 45 mg of lidocaine in parturients, so some caution is advised.

Local Anesthetic with Epinephrine
Injection of 15 µg of epinephrine as a test dose is a common technique for determining intravascular placement of epidural catheters. In healthy nonpreganant adults, either 10 or 15 µg of epinephrine produces statistically significant increases in heart rate and blood pressure. The mean maximal increase in heart rate after an intravenous epinephrine mixed with local anesthetics is 29-49 bpm and 29-46 bpm for 10 and 15 µg of epinephrine, respectively.19 Blood pressure also increases by 13-30 mm Hg and 18-33 mm Hg with 10 and 15 µg epinephrine, respectively.19

A widely accepted definition of a positive response to a test dose is a rise in heart rate 20 bpm above baseline. Since increasing the test does to 15 µg of epinephrine did not statistically or clincially improve the ability to detect intravascular injection, 10 µg of epinephrine could also serve as an effective test dose.

When performing test doses, symptoms of intravenous epinephrine injection can also be elicited from the patient. Common clinical symptoms and signs include palpitations, headache, chest pain, dysphoria, pallor, and tremulousness.

Local Anesthetic Alone
Local anesthetics alone have been recommended as agents in test doses when sudden increase in heart rate or blood pressure may be detrimental (e.g., severe preeclampsia). Lidocaine 100 mg is injected and subjective symptoms of local anesthetic toxicity are elicited from the patient (tinnitus, perioral numbness or tingling, metallic taste, dizziness). The sensitivity of this test ranges from 50-80%.

Air Test
In the air test for intravascular placement, 1 ml of air is injected through the epidural catheter while continuously listening to heart tones with a precordial ultrasound Doppler probe for 15 seconds. An investigation into the safety and efficacy of the air test in parturients determined that the test had an apparent sensitivity of 100% (it was positive whenever IV injection could be documented by any other means) and a specificity of 98% (the test was falsely positive in 6/281 patients).20 None of the 303 patients receiving the air test developed any complications from the technique.

Use of Epinephrine in the Presence of ß-Blockade
In the presence of ß-blockade, changes in heart rate cannot be relied on as an indicator of intravascular placement. Systolic blood pressure increased but heart rate did not when healthy nonpregnant volunteers were premedicated with a ß1-specific ß-blocker (esmolol) or with a nonspecific ß-blocker (propranolol) when 5, 10, and 15 µg of epinephrine were injected intravenously.19 An increase in SBP >15 mm Hg was recommended as criteria for determining intravascular placement. The investigators cautioned that these results may not be applicable to elderly individuals who may not respond with an increase in blood pressure.

Use of the Epinephrine Test Dose Under General Anesthesia
General anesthesia attentuates the hemodynamic response to an intravenous epinephrine test dose, although modification of the the response may depend on the depth and type of anesthetic. Positive criteria for intravascular injection under isoflurance and N2O anesthesia were determined to be a heart rate increase >8 bpm, an increase in systolic blood pressure > 13 mm Hg, an increase in disatolic blood pressure > 7 mm Hg, and an increase in MAP increase > 9 mm Hg.21
