Epidural Catheter Insertion Length
Threading the catheter too far into the epidural space may encourage the development of a number of complications, such as intravenous cannulation, kinking and occlusion of the catheter, curling and knot formation, curling around a spinal nerve, and passage into the anterior epidural space or out an intervertebral foramen resulting in patchy or unilateral anesthesia. Too shallow placement of the catheter may increase the risk of migration of the catheter out of the epidural space. In a prospective study that investigated optimal catheter insertion length, parturients were randomized to 2, 4, 6, and 8 cm catheter depth in the epidural space.16. Intravenous cannulation was more frequent at 8 cm. Catheters inserted at 2 cm were more likely to become dislodged, but were associated with significantly fewer cases of unilateral anesthesia. Catheters inserted at 2 or 4 cm required replacement more often than catheters inserted 6 or 8 cm because of more frequent dislodgement. Thus the optimal insertion length probably depends on the anticipated duration of epidural analgesia and perceived risk of catheter dislodgement. Although insertion at 2 cm decreased the incidence of unilateral block, incremental withdrawal of the catheter was successful in providing bilateral block 91% of the time. Intravenously placed catheters, however, were successfully withdrawn only 50% of the time.
