Puget Sound World Usability Day 2019

  • What: Puget Sound Usability Day 2019
  • When: November 14, 2019
  • Where: University of Washington, Seattle, WA

I graduated from the University of Washington in the summer of 2017. For the next year I wandered the abyssal professional no-mans-land between 'aspirant UX designer,' 'aspirant developer,' 'aspirant multimedia producer,' 'freelance web-dev & videographer,' 'open source geek,' and 'someone please interview me so I can finally pin down a mature, singular career path.' If employment were personal finance, my portfolio was a diversified, tediously sluggish climb through the gig economy before the knuckle-whitening bounces of reversion-to-mean came to resemble linear growth. UX was a hot field with fewer positions in the Seattle metropolitan area than software engineering, with competition further compounded by the influx of professionals from UX-adjacent careers—graphic designers, illustrators, artists, animators, front-end devs, psychologists, creatives, industrial designers, engineers, and the like.

This is what it's like to finally attend a UX conference as a full-time UX designer.

So even months into my employment as a UX designer at Infosys, I had sustained some incredulity. Am I really here? Am I really a UX designer?

It wasn't until my fellow UX folks at the company resolved to attend the annual Puget Sound World Usability Day conference that it hit me:

Whoah.

I've made it.

On this return to UW campus, I'm one of the fully employed professionals crowding the Husky Union Building for a badge-only UX summit among students trying to get their foot in the door, and not the other way around!

Human-Centered Design for a Robotic Future

by Krys Blackwood, JPL/NASA

This was a really content-rich talk, and Blackwood's experiences at the Jet Propulsion Laboratory (JPL) and NASA made for some great UX war stories:

The Europa Lander:

NASA recently sent a robot on a mission to take interesting pictures and collect & analyze geological samples from the surface of Europa, one of the moons of Jupiter. This robot was fully automated, as was necessary due to Europa's incredible distance from Earth, and would send a total of 48 kB of data back to NASA. It was estimated to take 90 hours for this 48 kB to travel from the lone robot on Europa to the United States. This tiny chunk of data will only be received once the mission is complete in 2028.

The Europa lander's artificial intelligence was developed and trained on Earth. The natural consequence of lander missions similar to this is that NASA engineers experience a degree of anxiety as to whether their robots are optimally performing their missions (if at all) while off in space, independently functioning as lone scientists in conditions inherently different from the ones they're trained in.

Krys found that this anxiety could be alleviated by allowing engineers to get really creative during the machine-learning phase of the lander's development. Just to prime their ability to simulate the most bizarre and novel situations on the alien wastes of Europa, the team trained the lander to identify and photograph tentacles, among other sci-fi pop culture emergencies.

The Deep Space Network: Patching Automation-Induced Operator Atrophy

The automation of the Deep Space Network (DSN) caused operator performance atrophy evident whenever tasks had to be performed manually. JPL/NASA experimented with various solutions to this issue of operator deconditioning, and found that intermittently shutting off the network's automation kept operators vigilant and skilled. This technique carried over to the airline industry, where enforcing brief moments of cancelled airplane automation keeps pilots 'fit' and passengers safe. The danger of automation-induced skill atrophy was one of the main focuses of The Glass Cage by Nicholas Carr (the 'glass cage' itself refers to the 'glass cockpit' of modern airplanes).

On Automation & Employment

The lecture began with Krys insisting that automation wasn't the job-eating beast depicted in 1980's sci-fi films, as robots excel at tasks us organics struggle with, like:

  • repetitive tasks
  • non-rewarding tasks
  • quick responses
  • precision
  • not panicking

... while they generally fail at tasks that involve human aptitude, such as:

  • adapting to unanticipated conditions
  • emergencies
  • understanding whether responses are socially or morally appropriate (citing Microsoft's 'Tay' incident)

Because of this, she believes that humans will always be needed; our automatons will depend on us to make up for their deficiencies and inform their values and missions.

At the end of the talk, when an audience member pressed her about her belief that automation won't decimate human labor, Krys admitted that automation has historically weeded out industries: "Automation will always destroy jobs, like refrigerators destroyed the ice industry." She remarked that it's our responsibility, though, as a village, to help those who lose their jobs adapt and find new work. Krys emphasized that the 'robotic future' is an inescapable and truly intriguing design problem for us to confront.

Usability in 20/20

by Ken Becker, Boeing

This was a retrospective on the historical trajectory of UX design; the issues we've long since solved (and seem trivial in 20/20 hindsight) and the usability faux paux still at-large to this day, like rotating menus, the antiquated information architecture of Hawaii's emergency response system (resulting in the 2018 North Korean missile crisis) and the indistinguishable design of elevator door closing and opening buttons.

Ken advocated we design for the 'self-explanatory' rather than the 'intuitive.' This is because intuition is a personal thing informed by experience, exposure and culture—all of which are subjective. Interfaces that are self-explanatory in nature are more usable and easier for us to pick up. I like this lesson, though we still need to determine how to create effective, self-explanatory designs without overwhelming users with too much explanation.

He also wants to bring back 'user-friendliness' into UX nomenclature. I think that's a good move. Dark patterns and persuasive design can be understood as user-hostile in some light, and reintroducing the term 'user-friendly' into parlance could guide designers towards solutions that are just that; friendly to our users.

The Future of Design Teams

by Joe Dyer, Projekt 202

"The Millennials are taking over," and it's time we evaluate the strengths of this new workforce and examine what it means to have predominately Millennial design teams.

One of the key traits that Dyer discussed was my generation's willful mobility. 71% of Millennials would like to work overseas, and 50% of us would like to work in a developing country. I think single-sentence statistics like this provoke an interesting line of deeper questioning. Cost-of-living might greatly factor into my generation's perception of mobility, rather than just our affinity for adventure. Would we still want to work in a developing nation for developing nation salaries? To what degree will remote work 'normalize' pay across national borders the design profession?

Dyer concludes that the blended workforce is here: 73% of teams will have at least one remote worker by 2020, and this statistic will only rise. Gallup has found that remote workers report higher engagement and job satisfaction. We'll see a greater rate of freelancers in the workplace. He mentions that these freelancers tend to struggle with 'teamwork' (the trait most desired by managers) according to managers, and that they report being willing to compromise pay for freedom and flexibility.

He anticipates that designers won't be as impacted by the encroach of AI and automation, due to our work's grounding in creativity and emotional intelligence.

Industries with the greatest growth that designers might be employed in include eCommerce, designing for seniors (to handle the growing population of retiring Boomers), video games (the industry has recently surpassed films in terms of annual revenue), and pet care and entertainment. He also sees great growth in the use of products using sensors, prosthetics, bioscience and kinesthesiology.

Designing Experiences That Connect Communities

by Vicki Haberman, Alaska Airlines

Haberman's talk revolved around the sentiment that we should take the responsibility of de-escalating the current atmosphere of digitally-grown loneliness and instant-gratification that our field has created; to 'do the right thing,' like Alaska Airlines. I agree.

We know that the efficacy of designs to maximize profitability, though, oftentimes has a correlation with levels of social isolation and vulnerability that enhances the predictability (and thereby direction) of user behavior. As experience designers, we're the vanguards that can discourage such predatory designs, but not all of us have the privilege to do the right thing when the right thing is often the financially or expediently wrong thing. Can any of us be the person to stand up and deny that consumers want same-day shipping, perpetual amusement, and to know what their exes are up to?

Haberman discussed the merger of Alaska and Virgin Airlines as the combination of two airlines that folks love. And that's undeniable--audience members during this segment's Q&A reported that they really love Alaska Airlines, and as a Pacific Northwesterner, I have an innate affinity for the airline too. Alaska has this cool, Spartan branding that seems to reflect our remote, frontiery, high-tech sensibilities. It's region-exclusive; it's the Cascadian airline. Alaska's really hard not to like.

For all the tales of shuttling bottled milk and passengers to the far reaches of Alaska in its early days and in spite of its excellent service (relative to that of other airlines), Alaska is still an airline and still has to compete with the likes of United, and with that comes environmental and psychosocial consequences. Last year a Horizon Air (a subsidiary of the Alaska Air Group) ground service worker hijacked an empty Alaska Horizon airplane from SeaTac airport, performed some spectacular aero-acrobatics and joy-rode the aircraft for 15 minutes, then crashed it into a nearby island, killing himself. When F-15s scrambled to make contact with the man, he cited his minimum wage. "We'll chalk it up to that," he said. A piece by one of the man's coworkers in The Stranger alleged that the situation for Horizon workers on 'The Ramp' was dehumanizing, woefully underpaid, militarised, and accented with a naturally high turnover and hostile cost-cutting mechanisms that appear inappropriate given that this event took place during the expensive purchase of Virgin Airlines by Alaska.

Given the recency of that incident, I found it hard to internalize this image of Alaska as connecting communities. They do some cool things; Haberman talked about how the airline arranges personal 'trial flights' for autistic folks and those with flight anxiety. My knee-jerk instinct for booking a flight is still Alaska for any excursion that requires more than a backpack. This bundling of the company with connotations of friendliness and goodwill, though, had me experiencing some cognitive dissonance.

The invitation to wield our design expertise to connect communities with our designs and heal the damages of division is a great challenge and something to bring forward in our future work. It's also a perfect segue into the lecture that followed.

Game Theory for Promoting Strategic Human Interactions to Create Designs that Matter

by Jeffery Dernbach, T-Mobile

Dernbach uses game theory and a bunch of whimsical illustrations to carry the audience to the hilarious reality that selling an ethical design to stakeholders, when understood through a thorough prisoner's dilemma matrix diagramming all conceivable product stakeholders and collaborators, is actually a matter of boosting our personal reputations as designers so people will take our design more seriously.

That isn't to say that other methods of persuasion aren't effective, but the fact is that they're relatively 'inefficient.' For example, logical appeals to each stakeholder would have to be crafted so as to court every stakeholder's personal self-interests. These interests, though, might not be shared or may even mutually exclusive across different stakeholders (for example, ethical designs may inhibit product profitability), so the overrall 'sum' of design-acquiscence resulting from playing to individual interests would be less than the sum of design-acquisence grown from predicating one's designs with a stellar reputation that encourages stakeholder cooperation and confidence.

I wish I could find a copy of Dernbach's prisoner's dilemma matrix to cite or re-sketch. I think this is a lesson that we implicitly know, but to see it logically laid out in such an entertaining format was a well-placed and light-hearted addition to the near-end of the conference.

Who Put GUI in my VUI?

by Paul Cutsinger, Amazon

Cutsinger shares the Amazon Alexa team's discovery that voice design patterns are fundamentally different than what visually-trained designers are used to, and different in surprising ways.

Idioms and verbal skeumorphism result in phrases like "turn up the volume," which only makes sense in English, and even then, doesn't really make sense ('up' is only something that can be 'turned' in reference to a dial). Amazon is 'evolving' Alexa to handle these complications and dialectic nuances by supplying the personal assistant with as much 'training data' as possible, and developing a wide gamut of text-to-speech voices for Alexa.

The primary challenge of Alexa skill creation is to design the skill to be extendable, while allowing users to access features in a way that closely mimics fluid conversation, rather than forcing users to navigate skills through menus, as in the cases of visual design and traditional answering-machine services. Given that voice design is a new practice, Cutsinger's seen designers experiment with different design methods to both maintain the human element of conversation while supporting the back-end 'logic' of the skills. Pixel-pushing isn't a concern in VUI design; the issue appears to be designing an efficiently traversible web of conversation that suspends the user's disbelief that they're talking with a semi-sentient machine.

Cutsinger reports that Alexa has over 100,000 skills (the VUI-equivalent of 'apps') at the moment.

I wonder what would make a neat Alexa skill. I haven't had the opportunity to play around with one of those machines yet.
